There is a limit to how much you will let yourself receive, and that limit was installed before you could question it.

I noticed this in a conversation with a friend who told me, with total sincerity, "I believe in God. I just don't believe God gets involved with money." She didn't recognize the double bind she was in. She believed God was good -- too good to touch something as dirty as money -- and she believed money was bad. Two beliefs, working together in perfect silence, keeping her broke and blocked.

Most of us carry a version of this. We were raised to believe money is the real source of security, and faith is a nice idea for Sundays. We'll pursue our art, we tell ourselves, when we have enough money to do it easily. And when will that be? We are always waiting for the account balance that gives us permission. But permission doesn't live in a bank. It lives in a decision.

We want a God that feels like a fat paycheck and a license to spend as we please. Listening to the siren song of more, we are deaf to the still small voice waiting in our soul to whisper: you are enough.

The Creator who designed the subatomic structure of reality, who wrote the physics that holds galaxies in orbit, is not confused about how to support your creative work. Look at creation itself. There is not one pink flower, or even fifty pink flowers, but hundreds. Snowflakes are the ultimate exercise in sheer creative glee. No two alike. This does not look like a source that runs out.

And yet we cling to our financial concerns as a way to avoid not only our art but our spiritual growth. Our faith is in the dollar. "I have to keep a roof over my head," we say. "Nobody's going to pay me to be more creative." We are awfully sure about that. Most of us harbor a secret belief that work has to be work and not play, and that anything we really want to do must be considered frivolous.

This is not true.

We are operating out of the toxic old idea that what the universe wants for us and what we want for us sit at opposite ends of the table. "I want to be a musician, but the universe wants me to wait tables," the scenario goes. This thinking is grounded in the idea that the source is a stern parent with very rigid ideas about what is appropriate for us. And you had better believe we will not like them.

This week, in your morning prayers, write about the god you do believe in and the god you would like to believe in. What would a nontoxic source think of your creative goals? Might such a source really exist? If so, would money or your job or your lover remain your higher power?

What we really want to do is what we are really meant to do. When we do what we are meant to do, money comes to us, doors open for us, we feel useful, and the work we do feels like play.


## The Luxury You Have Been Refusing

For those of us who have become artistically anorectic -- yearning to create and refusing to feed that hunger -- a little authentic luxury can go a long way. The key word is authentic. Because art is born in expansion, in a belief in sufficient supply, it is critical that we pamper ourselves for the sense of abundance it brings.

What constitutes pampering? It varies for each of us.

For one person, a pair of tweed trousers from the vintage store conjured up images of laughter and racy roadsters. For another, a single daisy on the nightstand told her life was abloom with possibility. Someone else found that the scent of real furniture wax gave him a feeling of safety, solidity, and order. Another found luxury in a magazine subscription -- a twenty-dollar gift that keeps giving for a full year of images and indulgence.

All too often, we become blocked and blame it on our lack of money. This is never an authentic block. The actual block is our feeling of constriction, our sense of powerlessness. Art requires us to empower ourselves with choice. At the most basic level, this means choosing to do self-care.

I know an artist of formidable talents, world-famous, assured a place in history for his contributions. He is sought after by younger artists and respected by older ones. Although not yet fifty, he has already received lifetime achievement awards. And yet he suffers. He continues to work, but at greater and greater cost to himself. Why does his life's work now feel so much like his life's work?

Because he has denied himself luxury.

Not the luxury of penthouse views and foreign sports cars. He has all of those. What he has denied himself is the luxury of time. Time with friends, time with family, time with no agendas of preternatural accomplishment. His many former passions have dwindled to mere interests. He tells himself he has no time to pass. The clock is ticking and he is using it to get famous.

Let me be specific about what luxury means in this context. Creative living requires the luxury of time, which we carve out for ourselves -- even if it is fifteen minutes for morning prayers and a ten-minute walk after work. Creative living requires the luxury of space, even if all we manage is one special bookshelf and a windowsill that is ours. Your artist is young at heart, and the young like things that are "mine." My chair. My book. My pillow.

Designating a few things as special and yours alone can go a long way toward making you feel pampered. Much of what we do in a creative recovery may seem silly. Silly is a defense our internal critic uses to squelch the artist child within. Beware of silly as a word you toss at yourself.

Creativity lives in paradox: serious art is born from serious play.


## Counting

For the next week, you will discover how you spend your money. Buy a small notepad and write down every nickel you spend. It does not matter what it is for, how tiny the purchase, how petty the amount. Petty cash is still cash.

Each day, date a page and count. What you bought, what you spent, where your money went. Be meticulous. Be thorough. And be nonjudgmental. This is an exercise in self-observation, not self-flagellation.

You may want to continue this practice for a full month or longer. It will teach you what you value in terms of spending. Often our spending differs from our real values. We fritter away cash on things we do not cherish and deny ourselves those things we do. For many of us, counting is a necessary prelude to learning creative luxury.

There is a deeper lesson here. Counting is not about deprivation. It is about alignment. When you know where your money goes, you know where your energy goes. And when you know where your energy goes, you can begin to redirect it toward the life you actually want instead of the life you have settled for.

All substance is energy in motion. It lives and flows. Money is a golden, flowing stream of concretized vital energy. When you block it with fear, when you hoard it out of scarcity, when you spend it unconsciously to numb yourself, you are damming the same river that feeds your creative life.


## Money Mythology

Complete the following phrases as fast as you can. Speed kills the inner critic because the critic operates through deliberation, through second-guessing, through the slow poison of "but."

People with money are ___. Money makes people ___. I would have more money if ___. My father thought money was ___. My mother always thought money would ___. In my family, money caused ___. Money equals ___. If I had money, I would ___. If I could afford it, I would ___. I am afraid that if I had money I would ___. Money is ___. Having money is not ___. In order to have more money, I would need to ___. When I have money, I usually ___. If I were not so cheap I would ___. Being broke tells me ___.

These phrases are not trivial. They are the coordinates of your financial mythology, the invisible code running beneath every creative decision you make about what you can and cannot afford to try.

Many of us equate difficulty with virtue and art with fooling around. Hard work is good. A terrible job must be building our moral fiber. Something that comes to us easily -- a talent for painting, say -- must be some sort of cheap trick, not to be taken seriously. On the one hand, we give lip service to the notion that the universe wants us to be happy, joyous, and free. On the other, we secretly think we deserve to be broke if we are going to be so decadent as to want to be artists.

Do we have any proof at all for these ideas?

Making art begins with making hay while the sun shines. It begins with getting into the now and enjoying your day. It begins with giving yourself some small treats and breaks. "This is extravagant but so is the Creator" is a good attitude to take when treating your artist to small bribes and beauties. Remember, you are the cheapskate, not the source. As you expect the universe to be more generous, it will be able to be more generous to you.

By seeking the Creator within and embracing our own gift of creativity, we learn to be spiritual in this world. We learn to trust that the source is good, that we are good, and that all of creation is good. In this way, we step out of the scarcity story and into the Ethereal Horizon, where abundance is not something we hope for but something we inhabit.
