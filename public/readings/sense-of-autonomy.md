This week we focus on artistic autonomy. We examine the ongoing ways in which we must nurture and accept ourselves as artists. We explore the behaviors that strengthen our spiritual base and, therefore, our creative power. We also look at the ways success must be handled so that we do not sabotage our freedom.

## Acceptance

I am an artist. As an artist, I may need a different mix of stability and flow than other people. A nine-to-five job may steady me and leave me freer to create. Or it may drain me. I must experiment with what works for me.

An artist's cash flow is typically erratic. I cannot determine my value or the value of my work solely by market value. My credibility lies with me, God, and the work itself.

Sometimes I will write badly, draw badly, paint badly, or perform badly. I have a right to do that to get to the other side. Creativity is its own reward.

As an artist, I must surround myself with people who nurture my artist, not those who over-domesticate it for my own good. Certain friendships enliven imagination; others deaden it.

Creativity is oxygen for the soul. Cutting it off makes us savage. The more I nurture my artist child, the more adult I am able to appear.

## Success

Creativity is a spiritual practice. It is not something that can be perfected, finished, and set aside. Plateaus of attainment are often followed by restlessness. We are asked to expand so that we do not contract.

As artists, we are spiritual sharks. If we do not keep moving, we sink.

Success can tempt us into formula, overcommitment, and safe repetition. But no artist can replicate a prior success indefinitely without risking artistic deadness. We must leave room for the projects that feed the soul, not only the ones that secure the business.

## The Zen of Sports

Most blocked creatives are cerebral. To effect a real recovery, one that lasts, we need to move out of the head and into a body of work. To do that, we often need to move into the body.

Walking, running, swimming, bicycling, dancing, riding, or any repetitive physical motion can function as moving meditation. The goal is not merely fitness. It is perspective, groundedness, and access to inspiration.

Exercise teaches the rewards of process. It teaches us that we are stronger than we thought. It teaches patience, stamina, and trust in our own inner resources.

## Your Artist's Altar

Morning pages are meditation, a practice that brings you to your creativity and your creator. In order to stay happily creative, it helps to stay spiritually centered through personal ritual.

Create a spiritual corner, ledge, shelf, or room. Fill it with things that make you happy: leaves, candles, stones, shells, pictures, flowers, symbols, music, and objects that speak to your senses. Let it remind you that your creativity is spiritual and embodied.

Your artist's altar should be a sensory experience. Your artist is a child of the soul and responds to images, texture, scent, color, and play.

## Tasks

1. Tape your own voice reading the Basic Principles and a favorite essay. Use the tape for meditation.
2. Write out your Artist's Prayer in longhand and place it in your wallet.
3. Buy yourself a special creativity notebook and list ten wishes in each of seven areas: health, possessions, leisure, relationships, creativity, career, and spirituality.
4. Inventory the ways you have changed since beginning recovery.
5. List five ways you will change as you continue.
6. List five ways you plan to nurture yourself in the next six months.
7. Plan one week of daily nurturing for yourself: one concrete loving action each day.
8. Write and mail an encouraging letter to your inner artist.
9. Reexamine your God concept. Does it limit or support your creative expansion?
10. List ten examples of personal synchronicity that support the possibility of a nurturing creative force.
